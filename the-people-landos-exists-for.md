# The People Landos Exists For

Landos was not built for everyone.

It was built for specific people — people in specific situations, facing specific problems that no existing system has solved. Understanding who those people are is essential to understanding what Landos is trying to do.

---

## The Farmer With No Deed

He has farmed the same land for thirty years. His father farmed it before him. His grandfather cleared it.

Everyone in the village knows it is his land. The boundaries are understood. The history is shared memory.

But there is no deed. No government document. Nothing that a legal system would recognize as proof.

When a company comes and says the land belongs to them — and they have a document, however fraudulent — he has no counter. His thirty years mean nothing to a court that only recognizes paper.

Landos exists for him. A system that encodes his community's knowledge into a permanent, incorruptible record. Proof that does not depend on paper, or on which officials can be bribed.

---

## The Woman in the Informal Settlement

She built her home in a neighborhood that grew up without official planning. Tens of thousands of people live in this neighborhood. Shops, schools, community organizations — a complete community, built by its residents over decades.

The government does not recognize these homes as legal property. The residents have no titles. No formal rights. They are, in the eyes of the law, occupying land that belongs to the state or to someone else.

When the city decides to develop the area, she and her neighbors are offered almost nothing. Because they own nothing — officially.

But they built this neighborhood. They have been here for decades. Their community knows who owns what.

Landos exists for her. A record that the community can create for itself, that does not depend on official recognition to be real, and that will exist as evidence when she needs it most.

---

## The Indigenous Community

They have lived on their territory for centuries. Their relationship to the land is not just economic — it is cultural, spiritual, foundational to who they are.

Their boundaries are not lines on a government map. They are known through oral tradition, through generations of living within them, through the community's own governance of its territory.

When a government or corporation wants their land, it looks for documents. There are none. So the territory is declared unoccupied, available, open for development.

Landos exists for them. A system that can encode territorial boundaries as the community understands them, that does not require translation into a legal framework designed by outsiders, and that creates a permanent record of occupation and ownership that cannot simply be declared nonexistent.

---

## The Family Caught in a Conflict Zone

They had a deed. They had a formal record. And then war came.

Records were destroyed. Offices burned. Officials fled. When the conflict ended, the records that proved their ownership were gone — and powerful people moved quickly to claim the land while the legal vacuum lasted.

Landos exists for the next family in this situation. A record that is not stored in any single office. That is maintained by thousands of computers around the world. That survives the destruction of every local institution because it exists everywhere at once.

A record that cannot be erased even when everything around it is.

---

## Who Landos Is Not Built For

This matters as much as who it is built for.

Landos is not built for the condo owner in a major city whose apartment is covered by a functioning legal system. That person has options. That person has lawyers, title insurance, government registries that work.

Landos is not built primarily for investors looking for returns, or for people who want to trade land as a financial instrument. Those uses may exist, and they may help fund the network — but they are not the mission.

The moment Landos optimizes for the investor over the farmer, for the institution over the community, for the wealthy over the vulnerable — it becomes another system that serves the people who need it least.

Every design decision in Landos is tested against this: does it make the system better for the farmer, the woman in the settlement, the indigenous community, the family in the conflict zone? Or does it make it better for someone else at their expense?

---

## Why This Framing Matters

It would be easy to build a blockchain land system that works well for wealthy, connected, technically sophisticated users — and offers the other 800 million people a watered-down version as an afterthought.

Landos is designed the other way. The primary user is the person with the least power and the most to lose. If the system works for her, it works for everyone. If it only works for the investor with a smartphone and a crypto wallet, it has failed at its core purpose.

The mission is not to build a better land registry for people who already have one.

The mission is to build a land registry for the people who have never had one.
