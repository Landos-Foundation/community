# Landos — The Woman With Land
## User Perspective Document v0.1.0

**Status:** Draft — thinking document  
**Date:** 2026-06-01  
**Purpose:** To answer the real questions a landowner would ask before trusting Landos. These are not marketing questions. They are the questions of someone who has been failed before and needs honest answers.

---

## The Person This Document Is For

She has land. She has lived on it, farmed it, raised her family on it. She has no formal deed. No government document that proves it is hers. She has heard about systems that were supposed to help people like her — and she has seen them fail, disappear, or be taken over by the same powerful people they were supposed to protect her from.

She is skeptical. She should be. She needs real answers.

---

## Her Seven Questions

### 1. "Will this make me a target?"

This is her first question and the most important one. If registering her land on a public system means someone powerful can see what she owns and come for it, she will not register.

**The answer:**

Landos is built with privacy at its core. The public blockchain never shows her real name, her real identity, or her precise location. What the public chain sees is a cryptographic identifier — a code that proves her claim is legitimate without revealing who she is.

Her real identity is stored separately, off-chain, with strict access controls. It is never visible to the public. It is never sold. It is never handed to governments or institutions without her consent.

She is protected by the same technology that protects financial privacy in the most secure systems in the world.

The longer answer is this: her land is already known to the people around her. Her neighbors know she lives there. The local power structures know. What Landos gives her is not invisibility — it is a record that cannot be altered or erased by those same power structures. The risk of registering is low. The risk of not registering is that she has no proof when she needs it most.

---

### 2. "How do I even start?"

She may not have a smartphone. She may have one but the internet is unreliable. She does not know what a blockchain is. She should not need to.

**The answer:**

She needs one thing to get started: access to a smartphone with a data connection, even briefly. It does not have to be hers.

In every community where Landos is active, there will eventually be a local person who helps others register — someone she already knows and trusts. In the early days, before those people are everywhere, she can find help through Landos's website, which has a simple guide and a chat assistant that walks her through each step in her language.

The registration process is designed for someone who has never used a blockchain. She does not need to understand how it works. She needs to be able to follow simple steps on a phone.

---

### 3. "What does it cost?"

She has no money to spend on registration fees. She cannot afford to travel. She cannot afford to lose a day of work.

**The answer:**

Nothing. Registering her land on Landos costs her nothing.

This is not a temporary promotion. It is a permanent design principle built into the protocol. The network is designed so that real landowners — people like her, who are present on their land, who participate in their community — generate the value that covers their own costs just by using the system. Her presence is her contribution.

There is no fee wall. There is no subscription. There is no hidden cost.

---

### 4. "What happens if I lose my phone?"

Her land record involves her phone. Phones break, get stolen, get lost. If her proof of ownership disappears with her phone, the system has failed her.

**The answer:**

Her land record is not on her phone. Her phone is a key to access it — but the record lives on the blockchain, which is maintained by thousands of independent computers around the world. No single device, no single government, no single organization controls it.

If she loses her phone, she can recover access. At the time she registers, she designates three people she trusts — neighbors, family members, people in her community with their own accounts. If she ever loses access, those three people can help her recover it. There is a waiting period to prevent fraud, and she is notified at every step.

In the worst case — she has lost everything and cannot reach anyone — there is a human support path. Real people, paid by the Landos Foundation, help her through it.

She will not lose her land because she lost her phone.

---

### 5. "Will my government recognize this?"

She is practical. A record that only exists somewhere she has never heard of does not feel real. She wants to know if this will actually protect her when it matters.

**The answer:**

Today, her government may not recognize it. That is honest.

But here is what is also true: her government's registry has already failed her. Her land has no formal record there. Landos is not replacing something that works — it is providing something that doesn't exist for her yet.

And the record Landos creates is real in a different, more durable way. It is permanent. It is timestamped. It is witnessed by her neighbors and anchored to the Bitcoin blockchain — one of the most trusted and permanent records in the world. It cannot be altered by a bribe. It cannot be erased by a corrupt official.

When someone challenges her claim, this record is her evidence. In community disputes, in local courts, in front of international organizations, in front of journalists — this record exists and it cannot be denied.

Governments that have failed their people will eventually find themselves relating to Landos on its terms. The countries that need it most have every reason to recognize a system that works better than what they have. The door is open.

---

### 6. "Who is behind this?"

Is this another NGO that showed up, made promises, and left? Another system designed by people who have never been to her village and do not understand her situation?

**The answer:**

There is a real person she can talk to. The Landos Foundation employs Community Ambassadors — people whose job is to be the human face of Landos, to travel, to meet communities, to answer this exact question with their own name and face. In the early years of Landos, before the network is large enough to sustain itself entirely through community, this is how trust is built — person to person.

There is also a Foundation — a small, publicly accountable organization that handles the human work the protocol cannot do yet: hiring support workers, paying them fairly, stewarding the early development. The Foundation's finances and decisions are public. It does not control the protocol. It cannot alter her land record.

And underneath all of that, there is a protocol — a set of rules running on thousands of computers around the world that no single person or organization controls. Not the Foundation. Not the creator. Not any government. The protocol is the bedrock. The people are the bridge to get there.

As the network grows, the human layer phases down. The Community Ambassadors step back. The Foundation winds down. What remains is the protocol — ownerless, permanent, belonging to no one and therefore belonging to everyone.

---

### 7. "What do my neighbors think?"

She will not adopt something her community does not trust. Their opinion matters more than any document.

**The answer:**

Her neighbors are not just bystanders in this system — they are part of it. When she registers her land, her neighbors sign off. Their cryptographic attestation is part of her record. They are witnesses. The community's knowledge of who lives where is encoded into the protocol as proof.

This means two things. First, her registration process is inherently communal — she brings her neighbors into it, and they bring their trust with them. Second, as more people in her community register, the network becomes stronger for everyone. Each new registration makes the community's collective record more complete and more defensible.

The Community Ambassador's role is partly to build this community trust before individual registrations begin — to answer questions publicly, to demonstrate the system, to let the community decide together before anyone is asked to commit individually.

---

## What She Needs to Believe Before She Registers

1. It will not make her less safe
2. Someone she trusts has used it or vouches for it
3. It costs her nothing
4. She will not lose her land if something goes wrong with her phone
5. The record means something — even if her government doesn't recognize it today

These are not technical requirements. They are human ones. Every design decision in Landos should be tested against whether it helps or hurts her ability to answer yes to all five.
