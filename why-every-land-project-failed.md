# Why Every Land Project Before Landos Failed

People have been trying to solve the land ownership problem for decades. Governments, NGOs, technology companies, international organizations — all of them have tried. Most have failed. The few that partially succeeded have not solved the core problem.

Understanding why they failed is essential to understanding why Landos is built the way it is. Every design decision in Landos is a direct response to a real failure that came before.

---

## The Projects That Failed

### Honduras and Factom (2015)

In 2015, the government of Honduras announced a partnership with a U.S. blockchain company called Factom to build a nationwide land registry on the blockchain. The announcement made headlines. The technology was real. The problem was urgent — land title fraud was rampant, with government officials routinely hacking the existing database to steal land for personal use or political allies.

The project never launched.

A 2015 audit of Honduras's land registry uncovered more than 700 corruption-related irregularities. The officials responsible for those irregularities were the same officials who had to approve the new system. They didn't.

**The lesson:** You cannot fix a corrupt system by asking the corrupt people who run it for permission. Any project that requires government approval to exist has already handed a veto to the people most threatened by it.

---

### Republic of Georgia and Bitfury

Georgia's government partnered with a blockchain company to put land records on a distributed ledger. Unlike Honduras, it actually launched. Records were hashed to the blockchain. The system ran.

It had almost no impact.

The reason: they digitized the existing records. Records that had been created under a Soviet-era system, through decades of political pressure and institutional corruption. Making those records immutable didn't fix them — it froze the injustice in place. If a record was wrong before it went on the blockchain, it was wrong permanently afterward.

**The lesson:** A blockchain is only as trustworthy as the data put into it. Digitizing a corrupt record doesn't create a trustworthy one. The problem is not the format of the record — it is how the record was created in the first place.

---

### Sweden and ChromaWay (2016)

Sweden built a working blockchain land registry system. The technology functioned. Banks were involved. The land registry authority participated. Tests were run successfully.

The project stalled because Swedish law requires physical pen-and-paper signatures on property contracts. One legal technicality — one line in an existing regulation — stopped a technically functional system.

**The lesson:** Any project that must operate within an existing legal framework inherits all of that framework's limitations. Laws written for paper cannot always accommodate technology — and changing laws takes years, requires political will, and can be blocked by anyone with an interest in the status quo.

---

### Bitland in Ghana (2014–present)

Ghana's land problem is severe — approximately 78% of land in the country is unregistered. Bitland launched in 2014 with genuine ambition: a community-driven blockchain registry that would finally address this.

More than ten years later, it has not delivered meaningful scale.

The reasons are multiple. Rolling blackouts made electronic systems unreliable. The challenge of reconciling new registrations against old, conflicting, incomplete records was far harder than anticipated. And even when communities successfully registered their land, there was no clear path from "registered on Bitland" to "legally recognized" or "economically useful." The record existed. It just didn't connect to anything that gave it power.

**The lesson:** Technology alone does not solve adoption. A record that exists in a system no one knows about or trusts is not meaningfully different from no record at all. And a record that cannot be used economically — that cannot back a loan or support a legal claim — has solved only half the problem.

---

### Cadasta Foundation (2015–present)

Cadasta is one of the more successful efforts — and its partial success reveals something important about the limits of documentation alone.

Since 2015, Cadasta has documented the land rights of over one million vulnerable people across 17 countries. They have worked with communities, NGOs, and governments. Their tools are genuinely useful. People who use them rate them highly.

But documentation is not protection. Cadasta captures a record of who claims what land. It does not make that record permanent. It does not make it incorruptible. It does not anchor it to anything that survives the failure of the organization maintaining it. And it does not automatically make the land economically active.

If Cadasta closes tomorrow, the records they hold are at risk.

**The lesson:** Capturing community land knowledge is necessary but not sufficient. The record needs to be permanent, decentralized, and economically meaningful — not just documented.

---

### World Bank Land Titling Programs

The World Bank has spent billions of dollars on land titling programs across Africa, Asia, and Latin America. The results have been consistently disappointing.

In Tanzania, a World Bank-funded program promised formal land certificates to villagers. Five years in, fewer than one-third of respondents had received their certificates. Even those who did receive them found that the promised benefits — access to credit, improved women's rights, reduced conflict — largely failed to materialize.

The deeper problem was design. These programs were built by outsiders who imposed individual title systems on communities with complex communal and customary land arrangements. A family that had shared rights to land for generations found those rights simplified into a single individual's name — often a man's, leaving women with less protection than before. The programs destroyed existing rights while creating new ones that didn't fit the social reality.

**The lesson:** Top-down solutions designed without understanding local context often make things worse. Land rights are embedded in social relationships, community memory, and customary practice. Any system that ignores this will fail the people it claims to help.

## The Pattern

Look across all of these failures and the same themes appear:

**They needed permission from the wrong people.** Every project that required government approval gave a veto to the people most threatened by transparency.

**They started with bad data.** Digitizing existing records without fixing how those records were created preserved the problems they were supposed to solve.

**They got stopped by legal systems.** Operating within existing legal frameworks meant inheriting existing constraints — constraints that took decades to create and cannot be changed quickly.

**They couldn't scale or sustain.** Organization-dependent systems are only as durable as the organizations running them.

**They stopped at documentation.** Creating a record is not the same as protecting it, making it permanent, or making it economically useful.

**They ignored how communities actually work.** Imposing outside frameworks on complex local arrangements destroys the very rights they were meant to protect.

## What Landos Does Differently

Landos is designed specifically to avoid each of these failure modes.

It does not ask permission. It exists regardless of whether any government approves it — the same way Bitcoin exists regardless of whether central banks approve of it.

It builds records from scratch through community validation, not by digitizing existing corrupt registries. The record is only as good as the community knowledge that creates it — and that knowledge is verified through multiple independent mechanisms before anything becomes permanent.

It does not depend on existing legal frameworks to function. It earns legitimacy through adoption and through the undeniable durability of its records — not through legal recognition that can be revoked.

It is not controlled by any organization. Landos will outlast any company, NGO, or government that might want to shut it down, because it runs on thousands of independent computers around the world simultaneously.

It makes records permanent through Bitcoin anchoring — the most durable record-keeping system ever created. A Landos record that has been anchored to Bitcoin cannot be erased even if every Landos node goes offline.

And it is built around communities, not imposed on them. Community hubs emerge from geography and human relationships. Customary and communal ownership is supported. The system reflects social reality rather than trying to replace it.

None of these choices are accidental. Every one is a direct response to something that failed before.
