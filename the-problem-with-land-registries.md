# The Problem With Land Registries

If you live in a country with a working legal system, your land ownership is probably recorded somewhere. A government office holds a document with your name on it. If someone challenges your claim, you show the document. The system works — imperfectly, expensively, slowly — but it works.

Most people in the world do not have this.

---

## What a Land Registry Is

A land registry is simply a record of who owns what land. In theory, it is a public service — a neutral, reliable source of truth that protects everyone equally.

In practice, it is only as trustworthy as the people who run it.

---

## How Registries Fail

**They are controlled by people who can be pressured.**

A registry is a database. A database has administrators. Administrators can be bribed, threatened, or replaced by whoever holds political power. When the people controlling the record also have an interest in who owns the land, the record stops being neutral.

This is not a hypothetical. It happens every day in dozens of countries. A farmer's record disappears. A community's territory is reassigned. A family loses land that has been in their name for generations — because someone changed an entry in a database.

**They were never built for everyone.**

Most land registries were designed to serve people who already had legal standing — people with documents, with lawyers, with the ability to navigate bureaucratic systems. The hundreds of millions of people living on land they have occupied for generations but never formally registered were simply left out.

Not because their claims are not real. Because the system was never built to see them.

**They are fragile.**

Paper records burn. Databases get corrupted. Governments collapse. Wars erase records that took generations to build. When the institution that holds the record disappears, so does the record — and so does everything it was supposed to protect.

---

## The Scale of the Problem

In 2000, the economist Hernando de Soto estimated that the world's poor hold approximately **$9.3 trillion** in land and property that cannot be used economically because it lacks formal recognition.

That number is almost certainly larger today.

This is not just a legal problem. It is an economic one. Land that has no formal record cannot be used as collateral for a loan. It cannot attract investment. It cannot be sold through formal markets. It cannot be passed to children in a way that legal systems recognize.

The land is real. The families on it are real. The value is real. But because there is no record, none of that value can be accessed.

**The absence of a record is one of the most powerful mechanisms that keeps people in poverty.**

---

## Why the Obvious Solutions Have Not Worked

The obvious answer seems simple: give everyone a land title. Run registration drives. Digitize the records. Connect the registries.

Many organizations have tried this. Most have failed. Here is why:

**They required government cooperation.** Any project that depends on existing institutions to function inherits all of those institutions' problems. Corrupt officials block systems designed to stop corruption. Political changes reverse progress. Systems built on government partnership can be dismantled by the next government.

**They digitized the wrong thing.** Putting a corrupt paper record on a blockchain does not make it trustworthy. It just makes it harder to change. If the original record was wrong — if it was created through fraud or coercion — digitizing it preserves the injustice.

**They did not solve the human problem.** Technology is only part of the answer. The harder part is: how do you establish who really owns a piece of land when there is no existing record? The answer requires local knowledge, community trust, and human judgment. No database can replace that.

---

## What a Real Solution Needs

A real solution to the land registry problem needs to do several things at once:

It needs to be **independent** — not controlled by any government, institution, or powerful actor who has an interest in the outcome.

It needs to be **permanent** — records that cannot be altered, erased, or lost when institutions fail.

It needs to be **human** — built around the social reality that communities know who lives where, and that knowledge is a form of proof.

It needs to be **accessible** — usable by people who have never interacted with a formal legal system and may not have reliable internet access.

It needs to be **economically meaningful** — not just a record that proves ownership, but one that allows that ownership to function as real economic capital.

No existing system does all of these things. That is why Landos exists.
