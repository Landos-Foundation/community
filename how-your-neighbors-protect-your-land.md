# How Your Neighbors Protect Your Land

Every community knows who lives where.

The farmers know whose field is whose. The families know who built which house. The elders remember who has occupied which land for how long. This knowledge exists whether or not any government has recorded it.

Landos is built on a simple idea: **that community knowledge is a form of proof.**

---

## The Problem With Individual Claims

If land ownership only depended on one person saying "this is mine," the system would be easy to abuse. Anyone could claim anything. The person with the most power — or the most money — would win every dispute.

Most formal land systems solve this by requiring documents: deeds, titles, government certificates. If you have the document, you own the land. If you don't, you don't.

But documents can be forged. Documents can be altered. And for hundreds of millions of people, the documents were never created in the first place.

Landos takes a different approach. Instead of relying on documents alone, it relies on **people who know the truth.**

---

## The Neighbor Quorum

When you register your land on Landos, your neighbors are part of the process.

You designate a group of people — people who live near you, who know your land, who can truthfully say that you are the rightful occupant. They confirm your claim using their own accounts on Landos. Their confirmation is cryptographically signed — meaning it is permanently attached to your record with their identity behind it.

This is called a **Neighbor Quorum**. It requires a minimum number of neighbors to agree — not just one — before a claim moves forward. A corrupt actor trying to fake a land claim would need to corrupt multiple independent people simultaneously. That is much harder than forging a single document.

---

## Why This Works

The neighbor quorum works because it encodes something that governments and databases often ignore: **communities have memory.**

A neighbor who has watched you farm your land for ten years knows the truth of your ownership in a way that no document can fully capture. When they sign their name to your claim — putting their own standing in the system behind it — they are doing something meaningful. They are making themselves accountable for what they attest to.

If a neighbor falsely confirms a claim they know to be untrue, they face consequences within the system. Their own reputation — their Personal Validation Score — is damaged. The community's ability to verify claims depends on the honesty of the people making attestations. Everyone has an incentive to be truthful.

---

## More Than Just Confirmation

The neighbor quorum is one layer of how community knowledge protects land on Landos. There are others.

**Community Memory** — when neighbors sign off on a claim, they can also add a written statement. These statements accumulate over time into a permanent record of the community's knowledge about that land. Years of attestations from multiple people create a history that is very difficult to fabricate.

**Proof of Habitation** — the system also tracks physical presence over time. A real landowner who lives on their land accumulates evidence of that presence through regular check-ins. This is not something a fraudster sitting far away can fake.

**Land Witnesses** — for important transactions, a neutral third party with no stake in the outcome can serve as a witness. Like a notary, but on-chain. Someone who confirms that a process was followed correctly and honestly.

Together, these mechanisms mean that establishing land ownership on Landos is not just about what you claim. It is about what your community can verify — and what the record of your presence over time confirms.

---

## What This Means for the Woman With Land

She has lived on her land for twenty years. Her neighbors have watched her farm it, build on it, raise her children on it. Everyone knows it is hers.

When she registers on Landos, her neighbors are not bystanders. They are participants. Their knowledge becomes part of her permanent record. The community's truth is encoded into a system that cannot be altered by whoever controls the local government office.

If someone powerful ever challenges her claim, she does not just have her word. She has the signed attestations of her neighbors. She has years of habitation records. She has a permanent record anchored to one of the most durable record-keeping systems ever created.

That is what her neighbors' participation gives her.
